Dual MA & MSc Thesis 2023 — 2025

From Vision to Cold Peace: Carter, Camp David and Militarisation

Dual MA/MSc in International History · Columbia University & LSE

A study of the Carter Administration's mediation of the Camp David Accords, built on three weeks of archival research at the Jimmy Carter Library, the American Jewish Committee and American Jewish Historical Society in New York, and the National Archives at College Park. The project traces how financial and military aid was explicitly leveraged as a mechanism to secure the durability of the peace agreement.

02 · Conferences & Talks

Presented work.

Conference 2024

14th Annual Consortium of African and Asian Affairs Symposium

Columbia University · New York

I had the pleasure of presenting at Columbia University in September 2024. The conference theme, "What is genocide, and what can universities do about it?", inspired two days of meaningful and timely discussions.

Drawing from my paper, I explored how contemporary wars reshape our understanding of conflict. A central tension in the analysis is the contrast between Clausewitzian conceptions of war — where armed conflict is understood as a rational instrument of state policy — and Mary Kaldor's "new wars" framework, which captures the identity-driven, fragmented nature of modern violence that increasingly blurs the boundaries between war, organised crime, humanitarian crisis, and genocide.

I also examined the evolving language around genocide itself — a term that, while crucial for mobilising international attention and action, carries significant limitations for humanitarian response. Legal definitions of genocide are both empowering and constraining: they can galvanise intervention, but they can also create thresholds that delay or restrict meaningful engagement. Cases like Rwanda, Darfur, and the DRC illustrate how this definitional tension plays out in practice, shaping, and some cases distorting, the international community's ability to respond.

EU-funded research Feb. 25, 2026

"Russia: its history, literature, and culture, have become pariahs"

Project 2022 · co-authored academic work

Dina Gusejnova and I interviewed Cambridge Emeritus Professor, Hubertus Jahn to discuss how Russia's war against Ukraine is transforming Russian Studies. The conversation covers covers the practical realities of conducting research when access to Russian archives is effectively closed, the case for decolonising rather than dismantling the field, and the political naivety Jahn sees in western manifestos calling for negotiation with Putin. This article was conducted as part of Project 2022, an EU-funded research initiative within the Eastern Academic Alliance documening the war's consequences on knowledge communities across Europe.
