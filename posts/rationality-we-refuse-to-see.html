The Rationality We Refuse to See.

On Beatrice Heuser, Arnold Toynbee, and the failure of imagination that keeps recurring at the heart of Western strategic thought.

I recently attended the annual LSE History lecture presented by Professor Beatrice Heuser, which sent me into a spiral through her work on international relations. One book in particular — Heuser's Flawed Strategy: Why Smart Leaders Make Bad Decisions (2024) — opens with a deceptively simple question: why do intelligent, well-informed leaders produce catastrophic strategic failures? Her answer is not cognitive bias in the conventional sense — not ego, not groupthink, not poor intelligence. The failure she identifies is epistemic. Leaders misread adversaries not because they lack information, but because they project their own values, logic, and rationality onto enemies who operate from fundamentally different frameworks. The adversary's behaviour is rational — coherent, internally consistent, purposeful. It is simply alien to the observer's own system of meaning. And the observer, rather than doing the harder work of understanding that system on its own terms, defaults to the conclusion that the other side is irrational.

Heuser anchors this argument in cases that are, in retrospect, almost embarrassing in their clarity. Zelensky's doubts about a full-scale Russian invasion in 2022. Chamberlain's confidence that he could do business with Hitler in 1938. Israeli intelligence dismissing warnings of the Hamas attack in 2023, fifty years after failing to anticipate the Yom Kippur attack. In each instance, smart people looked at an adversary whose behaviour was legible within its own framework and concluded: this is not something they would do. The error was not a failure of intelligence, but a failure of imagination — a refusal, or inability, to inhabit a different rationality.

Reading Heuser in proximity to Ryoya Nishida's PhD thesis on the 20th-century historian Arnold J. Toynbee, which I edited at the end of 2025, reveals that this epistemic failure has a much longer intellectual genealogy than Heuser's strategic focus suggests. Toynbee spent the better part of five decades making the same diagnosis at a civilisational scale.

Toynbee's great provocation — the one that made him beloved outside Britain and unfashionable within it — was his insistence that Western civilisation had no claim to universality. His anti-Eurocentric framework was a structural critique of the assumption that the West's historical logic was the neutral default, the benchmark against which other civilisations were measured and found wanting.

What Heuser identifies as a strategic pathology, Toynbee had already identified as a civilisational one.

Both are describing the same mechanism of projection: the refusal to reconstruct the adversary's worldview on its own terms, and the consequent misclassification of coherent, purposeful behaviour as disorder, irrationality, or threat.

Nishida's thesis frames Toynbee as a mondai hakken-gata — a "problem-finding type" intellectual, borrowing from the Japanese political theorist Masao Maruyama. Not a system-builder, but a thinker who identified problems that his contemporaries could not yet see. The problem Toynbee named was civilisational parochialism: the assumption, embedded in both scholarly practice and political life, that the Western frame was the universal one. It was not a popular diagnosis. His critique of European imperialism alienated him from the British establishment, and his non-Eurocentric world history was dismissed as utopian by the Realist school. His reputation declined at home in almost direct proportion to its rise abroad — embraced by Japanese historians, Afro-American intellectuals, and anti-colonial thinkers who recognised in him someone naming the structure of their own exclusion from the "rational" order.

Nishida's thesis explicitly contrasts Toynbee with Samuel Huntington — and this is where the connection to Heuser becomes sharpest. Huntington's Clash of Civilizations commits exactly the error Heuser documents, but at the level of grand theory. By essentialising civilisational difference — treating Islamic, Western, Confucian, and other civilisational blocs as constitutively alien to one another — Huntington forecloses in advance the possibility of understanding adversary logic on its own terms. His framework does not just predict conflict between civilisations. It produces the analytical conditions in which that conflict becomes unreadable as anything other than irrational hostility along pre-determined fault lines. Once you have categorised the other civilisation as fundamentally different, you have already done the epistemic damage Heuser describes. You have made their rationality invisible.

Toynbee refused cultural essentialism precisely because he understood that mutual intelligibility across civilisational lines was both possible and necessary. His critique of Spengler, his emphasis on civilisational interaction over civilisational fixity, his insistence that the West was one civilisation among many rather than the apex of a universal development — these were all moves against the same error. Not naïve universalism, but an insistence on the hard work of comprehension.

Heuser's contribution is to demonstrate that this failure recurs across history with remarkable consistency, in leaders who are, by any conventional measure, intelligent and well-informed. Toynbee's contribution — recovered and clarified by Nishida's archival work — is to show that the failure is not incidental but structural: built into the frameworks through which Western scholarship and statecraft have trained themselves to see the world.

The problem is not that smart leaders make bad decisions. It is that the categories available to them make certain kinds of understanding impossible. That is a harder problem to fix.
