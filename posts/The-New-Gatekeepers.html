The New Gatekeepers of the Creator Economy

The creator economy likes to describe itself as a rupture. The industry describes legacy media as hierarchical, credentialed, and slow. Podcasting on the other hand, is horizontal, accessible, and personality-driven, where the microphone moves from institutions to individuals and audiences choose hosts they trust rather than brands they inherit.

​​After six months at FlightStory working in production for the Diary of a CEO, I noticed the company's entire operating logic rested on a specific belief: that institutional gatekeeping has dissolved, and power has shifted to creators and their communities. This wasn't presented as a hypothesis but treated as achieved fact, embedded in editorial decisions, resource priorities, and how the company defined its competitive advantage.

But the reality of guest booking complicates this story.

Every production team knows the informal list. The “reliable” experts. The people who clip well, and speak in tight, monetisable soundbites. Names like Andrew Bustamante, Dr. K, Rangan Chatterjee, or Jesse Inchauspé circulate across shows. They become “the” voice on intelligence, geopolitics, behavioural psychology, health, glucose.

Guest booking is shaped by risk management. A repeat guest is predictable. They understand pacing, use simple language, and come with those “1, 2, 3, step frameworks” that generate clips and views. Booking them lowers uncertainty. For teams working against tight deadlines and performance metrics, that matters. But the effect is subtle recentralisation.

Authority becomes platform-dependent rather than institution-dependent. Instead of the New York Times columnist or the tenured professor, we get the podcast-native expert. Their legitimacy comes less from peer communities and more from audience familiarity and algorithmic circulation.

The creator economy promises democratisation. Yet its output still concentrates. It is simply redistributed along new lines: charisma, network density, platform literacy. There is also a feedback loop. Repetition confers expertise. If a guest appears on twenty major podcasts, they begin to feel authoritative irrespective of the depth of their underlying scholarship or experience. Podcasting does not invent expertise out of nothing – but it can amplify thin credentials into thick reputation.

The tension, then, is not between the elites and the people. It is between different mechanisms of gatekeeping.

Legacy media filters through editorial institutions. Creator-led media filters through booking teams, audience analytics, and brand alignment. Both systems select. Both systems exclude. The question is not whether podcasting has democratised media. In many ways, it has. The question is whether it has diversified epistemic authority or merely rebranded it.
