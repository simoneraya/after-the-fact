Reflections on “A Global and Transhistorical Perspective on AI: Labour and Rights”

On November 3rd, Committee on Global Thought at Columbia hosted a panel that I’m still turning over in my head two days later. The lineup — Professor Ahmed (Columbia), Professor Amrute (The New School), Professor Hassan (NYU), and Brooklyn-based intellectual Edward Onyweso Jr — came at AI from a direction you don’t often hear in mainstream tech discourse: not “what can it do?” but “for whom, and at whose expense?”

The panel’s framing was explicitly anti-technotopian, and that tension sat at the centre of everything. The familiar promise, that AI will liberate us from drudgery, deliver universal basic income, and resolve the climate crisis, goes largely uncontested in mainstream discourse. What the scholars exposed is how thoroughly that vision sidesteps the two questions that should be central: labour and rights. Who is actually doing the work that makes AI function, and do they have any meaningful say in the conditions under which they do it? Professor Amrute's contribution was the one that really rewired something for me. She introduced the concept of the "Majority World" — a term coined by Shahidul Alum — not merely as a geographic designation but as a numerical and political corrective: the people living outside the West are not a peripheral case, they are most of humanity. And it is precisely this majority that bears the sharpest end of AI's costs while remaining most absent from its governance. This framing is a direct challenge to the robot-replacement narrative that dominates mainstream AI coverage — the story that low-wage jobs will be automated away, that workers in the Global South are most exposed, and that this trajectory is more or less inevitable. Amrute's argument cuts against the grain of that story. The real dynamic isn't replacement, it's enmeshment: workers don't get phased out, they get folded into AI systems in ways that are structurally invisible and chronically under-compensated. The Kenyan Sama workers are the case she pointed to — people doing the traumatising labour of content moderation and data labelling that makes AI function at scale, yet who are entirely absent from the "future of work" conversation the tech industry is so fond of staging. This is a question of knowledge and power. Who gets to produce the concepts that structure how we understand AI? Who gets treated as a passive subject of technological change, and who gets to be an architect of it? The questions shape policy, investment, and whose labour gets projected where. Professor Hassan's discussion of AI in Africa made this concrete in a different register. He described a massive proliferation of foreign data and tech hubs across the continent — what he frames as a fourth industrial revolution — except Africa isn't really participating in it on its own terms. The infrastructure is there; the governance isn't. Data protection frameworks are thin or absent, and the entities configuring this landscape are international corporations and investment groups, not local communities or governments. Hassan calls this data colonisation, and the phrase does real work: it connects contemporary AI deployment to a longer history of extraction dressed up as development

One thing that didn't get as much airtime but lingered in the Q&A: the academy's own position in all this. Universities will always be consumers of AI and language models — they don't have the capital to build their own. But academic papers and publications have already been absorbed into the training data of models like GPT, largely without consent or compensation. What does that mean for intellectual labour? Who owns the distilled output of decades of scholarship when it becomes an input to a commercial product? I don't think anyone landed on an answer, but it felt like a thread worth pulling.

Public discourse on AI remains largely captivated by capability and spectacle — what these systems can do, how fast they are improving, what industries they might transform. The harder questions about labour precarity, data colonisation, and the distribution of AI's costs across the Majority World are still largely confined to academic circles and activist spaces. That gap feels significant. The frameworks for thinking critically about AI are already here, already sophisticated, already grounded in historical precedent — and they are being developed, in large part, by scholars whose expertise and geography place them outside the centres of the industry they are analysing. Whether those frameworks make it into the rooms where policy actually gets made remains, for now, an open question.
